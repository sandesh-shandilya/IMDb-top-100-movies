Top 100 Movies of All Time

1. The Shawshank Redemption
"The Shawshank Redemption" is a gripping portrayal of resilience and friendship amidst the harsh confines of Shawshank State Penitentiary. Tim Robbins' portrayal of Andy Dufresne, a man wrongly imprisoned, and Morgan Freeman's nuanced performance as his loyal friend Red, resonate deeply, making this film a timeless classic. Through its poignant narrative and unforgettable characters, "The Shawshank Redemption" reminds us of the enduring power of hope and the triumph of the human spirit.
2. The Godfather
"The Godfather" is an iconic cinematic masterpiece that delves into the intricate world of organized crime and family loyalty. Directed by Francis Ford Coppola and released in 1972, the film follows the Corleone family, led by Vito Corleone, portrayed by Marlon Brando, as they navigate power struggles and betrayal in the mafia underworld. With its unforgettable performances, rich storytelling, and timeless themes of power, honor, and betrayal, "The Godfather" remains a cornerstone of American cinema, captivating audiences for generations.
3. The Dark Knight
"The Dark Knight" is a 2008 superhero film directed by Christopher Nolan, featuring Christian Bale as Batman and Heath Ledger as the iconic Joker. Set in Gotham City, the film explores themes of chaos, morality, and the thin line between heroism and villainy. Ledger's portrayal earned him a posthumous Academy Award for Best Supporting Actor. It's hailed for its intense action, complex characters, and thought-provoking narrative, transcending the superhero genre to become a cultural phenomenon.
4. The Godfather Part II
"The Godfather Part II" continues the saga of the Corleone family with a gripping narrative that intertwines the rise of Vito Corleone, portrayed by Robert De Niro, and the reign of his son, Michael Corleone, played by Al Pacino. Directed by Francis Ford Coppola and released in 1974, the film masterfully explores themes of power, corruption, and the consequences of choices made in the pursuit of ambition. With its intricate storytelling, complex characters, and stunning cinematography, "The Godfather Part II" stands as a cinematic triumph, earning critical acclaim and solidifying its place as one of the greatest sequels in film history.
5. 12 Angry Men
"12 Angry Men" is a riveting courtroom drama where twelve jurors deliberate a murder case. Led by Juror 8, played by Henry Fonda, they confront biases and explore the nuances of justice. With its intense debates and minimalist setting, the film offers a timeless exploration of human nature and the pursuit of truth.
6. Schindler's List
"Schindler's List" is a 1993 historical drama directed by Steven Spielberg, based on the true story of Oskar Schindler, a German businessman who saved over a thousand Polish Jews during the Holocaust. The film is renowned for its powerful portrayal of the atrocities of the Holocaust and the extraordinary acts of heroism and compassion amidst the darkness of war. With its haunting imagery, brilliant performances, and profound message of hope and humanity, "Schindler's List" is considered one of the greatest films ever made, winning seven Academy Awards, including Best Picture and Best Director.
7. The Lord of the Rings : The Return of the King
"Return of the King" is the final installment of Peter Jackson's "The Lord of the Rings" trilogy. Released in 2003, it concludes Frodo Baggins' journey to destroy the One Ring. The film is celebrated for its epic battles, emotional depth, and stunning visuals, winning 11 Academy Awards, including Best Picture.
8. Pulp Fiction
"Pulp Fiction" is a 1994 neo-noir crime film directed by Quentin Tarantino, known for its non-linear narrative and eclectic ensemble cast. The film weaves together interconnected stories of mobsters, boxers, and hitmen in Los Angeles. With its witty dialogue, stylish direction, and iconic scenes, "Pulp Fiction" revitalized independent cinema and became a cultural phenomenon. It won the Palme d'Or at the Cannes Film Festival and received widespread acclaim for its bold storytelling and unconventional approach to filmmaking.
9. The Lord of the Rings : The Fellowship of the Ring
"The Fellowship of the Ring" is the first film in Peter Jackson's "The Lord of the Rings" trilogy. Released in 2001, it follows Frodo Baggins and his companions as they embark on a perilous quest to destroy the One Ring. Praised for its visuals and characters, the film captivated audiences worldwide.
10. The Good, the Bad, the Ugly
"The Good, the Bad and the Ugly" is a 1966 epic Spaghetti Western directed by Sergio Leone, starring Clint Eastwood, Lee Van Cleef, and Eli Wallach. Set during the American Civil War, it follows three gunslingers - Blondie (the Good), Angel Eyes (the Bad), and Tuco (the Ugly) - as they search for buried Confederate gold. Known for its iconic score by Ennio Morricone, sweeping cinematography, and intense showdowns, the film has become a classic in the Western genre, celebrated for its gripping storytelling and memorable characters.
11. Dune : Part II
"Dune 2" is the highly anticipated continuation of Denis Villeneuve's epic adaptation of Frank Herbert's classic novel. Building upon the success of the first installment, the sequel promises to delve deeper into the rich world of Arrakis, delivering captivating storytelling and breathtaking visuals. As fans eagerly await further details, "Dune 2" is poised to captivate audiences once again with its immersive universe and compelling characters.
12. Forrest Gump
"Forrest Gump" is a 1994 comedy-drama starring Tom Hanks. The film follows Forrest, a man with a low IQ, as he unintentionally influences key historical events. Known for its heartwarming story and memorable characters, it won multiple Academy Awards, including Best Picture and Best Actor for Hanks.
13. The Lord of the Rings : The Two Towers
"The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers" is a 2002 epic fantasy film directed by Peter Jackson, the second installment in "The Lord of the Rings" film trilogy. It follows the journey of Frodo Baggins and the Fellowship of the Ring as they continue their quest to destroy the One Ring. Along the way, they encounter new allies and adversaries, including the creature Gollum and the evil wizard Saruman. With its stunning visuals, epic battles, and memorable characters, "The Two Towers" is widely regarded as one of the greatest fantasy films of all time.
14. Fight Club
"Fight Club" is a 1999 psychological thriller directed by David Fincher, starring Brad Pitt and Edward Norton. It follows an unnamed narrator who forms an underground fight club with the enigmatic Tyler Durden. The film explores themes of masculinity, consumerism, and identity, with a twist ending that challenges viewers' perceptions. "Fight Club" gained a cult following for its bold storytelling, intense performances, and subversive themes, leaving a lasting impact on popular culture.
15. Inception
"Inception" is a 2010 sci-fi thriller directed by Christopher Nolan. Starring Leonardo DiCaprio, it follows a skilled thief who enters dreams to steal secrets. With stunning visuals and a mind-bending plot, the film captivated audiences, becoming a modern classic.
16. Star Wars : Episode V - The Empire Strikes Back
"Star Wars: Episode V - The Empire Strikes Back" is a 1980 space opera directed by Irvin Kershner, the second installment in the original Star Wars trilogy. The film follows the Rebel Alliance's struggle against the tyrannical Galactic Empire, led by Darth Vader, as they face new challenges and revelations. With its iconic characters, thrilling action sequences, and unforgettable plot twists, "The Empire Strikes Back" is widely regarded as one of the greatest sequels of all time. It explores themes of good versus evil, the power of hope, and the complexities of the Force, leaving a lasting impact on popular culture and earning critical acclaim for its storytelling and visual effects.
17. The Matrix
"The Matrix" is a 1999 science fiction film directed by the Wachowskis, starring Keanu Reeves as Neo, a computer programmer who discovers the truth about reality: that it is a simulated world created by intelligent machines to control humanity. With its groundbreaking visual effects, innovative storytelling, and philosophical themes, "The Matrix" revolutionized the action genre and became a cultural phenomenon. The film explores concepts of identity, free will, and the nature of reality, captivating audiences with its mind-bending premise and adrenaline-pumping action sequences.
18. Goodfellas
"Goodfellas" is a 1990 crime film directed by Martin Scorsese, based on the true story of mobster Henry Hill. Starring Ray Liotta, Robert De Niro, and Joe Pesci, the film follows Hill's rise through the ranks of the Mafia and his eventual downfall. Praised for its intense performances, stylish direction, and memorable soundtrack, "Goodfellas" is considered one of the greatest films in the gangster genre. It received widespread critical acclaim and was nominated for multiple Academy Awards, winning Best Supporting Actor for Pesci's electrifying performance.
19. One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest
"One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest" is a 1975 drama film directed by Miloš Forman, starring Jack Nicholson. It follows Randle McMurphy, who feigns insanity to serve his sentence in a mental institution. The film explores themes of individualism and oppression, winning five Academy Awards, including Best Picture and Best Actor for Nicholson.
20. Se7en
"Se7en" is a 1995 psychological thriller directed by David Fincher, starring Brad Pitt and Morgan Freeman. The film follows two detectives as they hunt down a serial killer who uses the seven deadly sins as his modus operandi. With its dark and gritty atmosphere, compelling performances, and shocking twists, "Se7en" is celebrated for its suspenseful storytelling and exploration of morality and human nature. It has become a cult classic in the thriller genre, leaving a lasting impact on audiences with its disturbing yet thought-provoking narrative.
21. It's a Wonderful Life
"It's a Wonderful Life" is a 1946 Christmas fantasy drama directed by Frank Capra, starring James Stewart as George Bailey. The film follows George, a small-town banker on the verge of suicide, who is visited by a guardian angel named Clarence. Clarence shows George how different life in his community would be if he had never been born, leading George to realize the profound impact he has had on the lives of others. "It's a Wonderful Life" is celebrated for its heartwarming message about the value of life, family, and community, becoming a beloved holiday classic cherished by audiences for generations.
22. Interstellar
"Interstellar" is a 2014 science fiction film directed by Christopher Nolan, starring Matthew McConaughey, Anne Hathaway, and Jessica Chastain. The story follows a group of astronauts who embark on a journey through a wormhole near Saturn in search of a new habitable planet for humanity. As they navigate the complexities of space and time, they confront existential questions about love, sacrifice, and the survival of the human race. With its stunning visual effects, ambitious storytelling, and emotional depth, "Interstellar" captivated audiences and sparked discussions about the nature of space exploration and the future of humanity.
23. Seven Samurai
"Seven Samurai" is a 1954 Japanese epic samurai film directed by Akira Kurosawa, often regarded as one of the greatest films ever made. Set in 16th century Japan, the story follows a village that hires seven ronin (masterless samurai) to protect them from bandits who threaten to raid their harvest. The film explores themes of honor, sacrifice, and the complexity of human relationships as the samurai train the villagers to defend themselves. With its compelling characters, masterful direction, and groundbreaking cinematography.
24. The Silence of the Lambs
"The Silence of the Lambs" is a 1991 psychological horror film directed by Jonathan Demme. It follows FBI trainee Clarice Starling, played by Jodie Foster, as she seeks the help of incarcerated cannibalistic serial killer Dr. Hannibal Lecter, portrayed by Anthony Hopkins, to catch another serial killer known as Buffalo Bill. The film won multiple Academy Awards, including Best Picture, Best Director, Best Actor, Best Actress, and Best Adapted Screenplay, making it a classic in the thriller genre.
25. Saving Private Ryan
"Saving Private Ryan" is a 1998 war film directed by Steven Spielberg, starring Tom Hanks as Captain John Miller. The story follows a group of soldiers tasked with finding and bringing home Private James Ryan, whose three brothers have been killed in action during World War II. Known for its realistic portrayal of combat and emotional depth, the film is praised for its intense battle sequences and powerful performances. "Saving Private Ryan" received critical acclaim and won five Academy Awards, including Best Director for Spielberg, cementing its status as one of the greatest war films ever made.
26. City of God
"City of God" is a 2002 Brazilian crime film directed by Fernando Meirelles and Kátia Lund. The story is set in the favelas of Rio de Janeiro and follows the lives of various characters, including two young boys named Rocket and Lil Dice, as they navigate the dangerous world of organized crime and drug trafficking. The film is known for its gritty realism, kinetic cinematography.
27. Life is Beautiful
"Life is Beautiful" is a 1997 Italian tragicomedy film directed by and starring Roberto Benigni. The story follows Guido Orefice, a Jewish Italian man, who uses his humor and imagination to shield his young son from the horrors of a Nazi concentration camp during World War II. Through love and laughter, Guido attempts to preserve his son's innocence amidst the brutality of their circumstances.
28. The Green Mile
"The Green Mile" is a 1999 fantasy crime drama film directed by Frank Darabont, based on the novel by Stephen King. Set in the 1930s, the story revolves around Paul Edgecomb, a prison guard played by Tom Hanks, who oversees Death Row at a Louisiana penitentiary. When a gentle giant named John Coffey, portrayed by Michael Clarke Duncan, is sentenced to death for the murder of two girls, Paul discovers that John possesses extraordinary healing abilities and a kind-hearted nature.
29. Terminator 2 : Judgement Day
"Terminator 2: Judgment Day" is a 1991 science fiction action film directed by James Cameron, starring Arnold Schwarzenegger as the Terminator and Linda Hamilton as Sarah Connor. The film is a sequel to the 1984 film "The Terminator" and follows Sarah Connor and her son John as they are pursued by a more advanced and deadly Terminator, the T-1000, played by Robert Patrick. With the help of a reprogrammed Terminator, they attempt to prevent Judgment Day, a future nuclear apocalypse initiated by a rogue AI system known as Skynet.
30. Star Wars : Episode IV - A New Hope
"Star Wars: Episode IV - A New Hope" is a 1977 epic space opera film written and directed by George Lucas. It is the first installment in the original Star Wars trilogy and follows the journey of Luke Skywalker, played by Mark Hamill, as he joins forces with Princess Leia, Han Solo, and the Rebel Alliance to defeat the tyrannical Galactic Empire and its evil leader, Darth Vader. The film introduced audiences to iconic characters such as Luke Skywalker, Princess Leia, Darth Vader, and Obi-Wan Kenobi, as well as groundbreaking special effects and an unforgettable score by John Williams.
31. Back to the Future
"Back to the Future" is a 1985 science fiction comedy film directed by Robert Zemeckis, starring Michael J. Fox as Marty McFly and Christopher Lloyd as Dr. Emmett "Doc" Brown. The story follows Marty, a teenager who accidentally travels back in time to 1955 in a time-traveling DeLorean invented by Doc Brown. There, Marty encounters his parents as teenagers and inadvertently interferes with their budding romance, threatening his own existence.
32. Spirited Away
"Spirited Away" is a 2001 Japanese animated fantasy film written and directed by Hayao Miyazaki and produced by Studio Ghibli. The story follows Chihiro, a young girl who becomes trapped in a mysterious and magical world after her parents are transformed into pigs by an enchanted bathhouse for spirits. As she navigates this strange realm, Chihiro encounters various creatures and spirits, embarking on a journey of self-discovery and growth.
33. The Pianist
"The Pianist" is a 2002 biographical war film directed by Roman Polanski, based on the memoir of the same name by Polish-Jewish pianist and composer Władysław Szpilman. The film stars Adrien Brody as Szpilman, a Jewish musician who survives the Holocaust in Nazi-occupied Warsaw during World War II.
34. Spider-Man : Across the Spider-Verse
"Spider-Man: Across the Spider-Verse" is an upcoming animated superhero film directed by Joaquim Dos Santos, Kemp Powers, and Justin K. Thompson. It is the sequel to the critically acclaimed "Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse" (2018). The film continues the story of Miles Morales, a young Spider-Man from an alternate dimension, as he encounters various versions of Spider-Man from different dimensions.
35. Parasite
"Parasite" is a 2019 South Korean black comedy thriller film directed by Bong Joon-ho. The story follows the impoverished Kim family as they con their way into working for the wealthy Park family, infiltrating their household one by one. As the Kims' deception deepens, tensions rise, leading to unexpected twists and turns that culminate in a shocking climax.
36. Psycho
"Psycho" is a 1960 psychological horror film directed by Alfred Hitchcock, based on the novel of the same name by Robert Bloch. The film stars Anthony Perkins as Norman Bates, the owner of the Bates Motel, and Janet Leigh as Marion Crane, a secretary who finds herself entangled in a web of murder and deception after stealing money from her employer.
37. Gladiator
"Gladiator" is a 2000 epic historical drama film directed by Ridley Scott, starring Russell Crowe, Joaquin Phoenix, and Connie Nielsen. Set in ancient Rome, the film follows the story of Maximus Decimus Meridius, a Hispano-Roman general who is betrayed by Commodus, the ambitious son of Emperor Marcus Aurelius. Maximus is sold into slavery and becomes a gladiator, seeking revenge against Commodus while striving to restore Rome to its former glory.
38. The Lion King
"The Lion King" is a 1994 animated musical film produced by Walt Disney Feature Animation. Directed by Roger Allers and Rob Minkoff, it tells the story of a young lion named Simba who must reclaim his rightful place as king after the murder of his father, Mufasa, by his uncle Scar. Simba's journey is filled with adventure, friendship, and self-discovery as he learns about responsibility and embraces his destiny.
39. Leon: The Professional
"Leon: The Professional" is a 1994 action thriller film directed by Luc Besson, starring Jean Reno, Gary Oldman, and a young Natalie Portman. The story revolves around Leon, a professional hitman (played by Reno), who reluctantly takes in Mathilda (Portman), a 12-year-old girl, after her family is murdered by corrupt DEA agents led by Stansfield (Oldman). As Leon trains Mathilda in the ways of his profession, their unlikely bond deepens, and they seek revenge against those who wronged them.
40. The Departed
"The Departed" is a 2006 crime thriller directed by Martin Scorsese, starring Leonardo DiCaprio, Matt Damon, Jack Nicholson, and Mark Wahlberg. Set in Boston, the film follows the story of two moles, one working for the Massachusetts State Police (DiCaprio) and the other for a notorious Irish-American mob boss (Damon), who infiltrate each other's organizations. As tensions rise and the stakes get higher, both moles race to uncover each other's identities before it's too late.
41. American History X
"American History X" is a 1998 drama film directed by Tony Kaye and starring Edward Norton and Edward Furlong. It tells the story of Derek Vinyard (Norton), a former neo-Nazi skinhead who tries to prevent his younger brother, Danny (Furlong), from following in his footsteps after being released from prison. The film explores themes of racism, hate, redemption, and the impact of violence on individuals and communities.
42. Whiplash
"Prestige" is a 2006 psychological thriller directed by Christopher Nolan. It stars Hugh Jackman and Christian Bale as rival magicians in 19th-century London. The film delves into themes of obsession, sacrifice, and deception, keeping viewers on the edge with its intricate plot twists. Known for its stellar performances and mind-bending narrative, "Prestige" remains a favorite among fans of Nolan's work and psychological thrillers.
43. The Prestige
"The Prestige" is a 2006 psychological thriller film directed by Christopher Nolan, starring Hugh Jackman and Christian Bale. Set in London during the late 19th century, the film follows the intense rivalry between two magicians, Robert Angier (Jackman) and Alfred Borden (Bale). As their competition escalates, they become entangled in a web of deception, obsession, and revenge.
44. Grave of the Fireflies
"Grave of the Fireflies" is a 1988 animated film directed by Isao Takahata and produced by Studio Ghibli. Set in Japan during World War II, the film follows the story of two siblings, Seita and Setsuko, as they struggle to survive in the aftermath of the firebombing of Kobe. With their mother killed and their father serving in the navy, Seita and Setsuko are left to fend for themselves amidst the devastation of war. The film poignantly explores themes of loss, resilience, and the innocence of childhood in the face of tragedy.
45. Harakiri
"Harakiri" is a 1962 Japanese samurai film directed by Masaki Kobayashi. Set in the early 17th century, during the Edo period, the film tells the story of a poverty-stricken samurai named Tsugumo Hanshiro who arrives at the estate of a feudal lord requesting to commit ritual suicide, known as harakiri.
46. The Usual Suspects
"The Usual Suspects" is a 1995 neo-noir mystery film directed by Bryan Singer and written by Christopher McQuarrie. The film stars Stephen Baldwin, Gabriel Byrne, Benicio del Toro, Kevin Pollak, Chazz Palminteri, Kevin Spacey, and Pete Postlethwaite. The story follows the interrogation of a small-time conman named Roger "Verbal" Kint (played by Kevin Spacey), who recounts the events leading up to a deadly gun battle and explosion on a ship docked in San Pedro, California. As Verbal tells his story, the plot thickens, revealing layers of deception, manipulation, and betrayal among a group of criminals known as "the usual suspects."
47. Casablanca
"Casablanca" is a 1942 romantic drama film directed by Michael Curtiz, starring Humphrey Bogart and Ingrid Bergman. Set during World War II, the film follows the story of Rick Blaine, an American expatriate who runs a nightclub in Casablanca, Morocco. His world is turned upside down when his former lover, Ilsa Lund, re-enters his life, seeking his help to escape from the Nazis with her husband, Victor Laszlo, a Czech resistance leader.
48. The Intouchables
"The Intouchables" is a 2011 French comedy-drama film directed by Olivier Nakache and Éric Toledano. It follows the unlikely friendship between Philippe, a wealthy quadriplegic, and Driss, his caregiver from a disadvantaged background. The film explores themes of friendship, compassion, and the transformative power of human connection. It was a critical and commercial success, praised for its heartwarming story and strong performances.
49. Cinema Paradiso
"Cinema Paradiso" is a 1988 Italian drama film directed by Giuseppe Tornatore. The story revolves around Salvatore, a successful filmmaker who reflects on his childhood and his friendship with the projectionist at the local cinema, Alfredo. Through their bond, Salvatore discovers his passion for filmmaking. The film celebrates the magic of cinema and the nostalgia associated with it, as well as the importance of friendship, love, and the pursuit of dreams. "Cinema Paradiso" won the Academy Award for Best Foreign Language Film and is widely regarded as a classic of Italian cinema.
50. Modern Times
"Modern Times" is a classic comedy film written and directed by Charlie Chaplin, released in 1936. Chaplin also stars in the film as his iconic character, The Tramp. Set during the Great Depression, the film follows The Tramp as he struggles to survive in a modern industrialized society. Throughout the film, he encounters various comedic situations and mishaps while working in factories and trying to find employment. "Modern Times" is not only a hilarious satire of the dehumanizing effects of industrialization and capitalism but also a poignant commentary on social and economic issues of the time.
51. Rear Window
"Rear Window" is a 1954 American mystery thriller film directed by Alfred Hitchcock and starring James Stewart and Grace Kelly. The film is based on Cornell Woolrich's 1942 short story "It Had to Be Murder."
52. Once Upon a Time in the West
"Once Upon a Time in the West" is a 1968 epic Spaghetti Western film directed by Sergio Leone, known for his influential contributions to the genre. The film stars Henry Fonda, Claudia Cardinale, Jason Robards, and Charles Bronson.
53. Alien
"Alien" is a 1979 science fiction horror film directed by Ridley Scott. It follows the crew of the commercial space tug Nostromo who encounter a deadly extraterrestrial creature on a distant planet. The film stars Sigourney Weaver as Ellen Ripley, alongside Tom Skerritt, Veronica Cartwright, Harry Dean Stanton, John Hurt, Ian Holm, and Yaphet Kotto.
54. City Lights
"City Lights" is a 1931 American silent romantic comedy-drama film directed by and starring Charlie Chaplin. It is widely regarded as one of Chaplin's greatest and most enduring works. The film tells the story of the Tramp (played by Chaplin), a lovable and resilient vagrant who falls in love with a blind flower girl (played by Virginia Cherrill). Despite his own impoverished circumstances, the Tramp dreams of helping her regain her sight and live a better life.
55. 12th Fail
Based on the true story of IPS officer Manoj Kumar Sharma, 12th Fail sheds limelight on fearlessly embracing the idea of restarting the academic journey despite the setbacks and challenges and reclaiming one’s destiny at a place where millions of students attempt the world’s toughest competitive exam: UPSC.
56. Apocalypse Now
"Apocalypse Now" is a 1979 American epic war film directed, produced, and co-written by Francis Ford Coppola. The film is loosely based on Joseph Conrad's novella "Heart of Darkness" and follows the journey of Captain Benjamin L. Willard, played by Martin Sheen, as he travels deep into the Cambodian jungle during the Vietnam War to assassinate a renegade Special Forces officer named Colonel Kurtz, played by Marlon Brando, who has gone rogue and established himself as a godlike figure among a local tribe.
57. Django Unchained
"Django Unchained" is a 2012 American revisionist Western film written and directed by Quentin Tarantino. Set in the American South in the 1850s, the film stars Jamie Foxx as Django, a freed slave who becomes a bounty hunter, and Christoph Waltz as Dr. King Schultz, a German dentist turned bounty hunter who becomes Django's mentor and partner.
58. Memento
"Memento" is a 2000 psychological thriller film directed by Christopher Nolan. The film is known for its innovative narrative structure, which unfolds in a non-linear fashion, and its exploration of themes such as memory, perception, and identity.
59. WALL-E
"WALL-E" is a 2008 animated science fiction film produced by Pixar Animation Studios and directed by Andrew Stanton. The film follows the story of WALL-E, a waste-collecting robot left behind on an abandoned, desolate Earth after humans have evacuated the planet due to environmental degradation.
60. Raiders of the Lost Ark
"Raiders of the Lost Ark" is a 1981 action-adventure film directed by Steven Spielberg and produced by George Lucas. It is the first installment in the Indiana Jones film series and stars Harrison Ford as the titular character, an archaeologist and adventurer known for his trademark fedora and bullwhip.
61. The Lives of Others
"The Lives of Others" is a 2006 German drama film directed by Florian Henckel von Donnersmarck. The film is set in East Berlin in 1984 and follows the story of a Stasi (secret police) officer named Gerd Wiesler, played by Ulrich Mühe, who is assigned to spy on a playwright named Georg Dreyman, played by Sebastian Koch, and his actress girlfriend Christa-Maria Sieland, played by Martina Gedeck.
62. Sunset Blvd.
"Sunset Boulevard" is a 1950 American film noir directed by Billy Wilder and starring William Holden, Gloria Swanson, Erich von Stroheim, and Nancy Olson. The film is known for its dark and cynical portrayal of Hollywood and its examination of the relationship between fame, wealth, and sanity.
63. Paths of Glory
"Paths of Glory" is a 1957 anti-war film directed by Stanley Kubrick and based on the novel of the same name by Humphrey Cobb. Set during World War I, the film stars Kirk Douglas as Colonel Dax, a French army officer who defends three soldiers accused of cowardice in a doomed mission.
64. Avengers : Infinity War
"Avengers: Infinity War" is a 2018 superhero film directed by Anthony and Joe Russo and produced by Marvel Studios. It is the nineteenth film in the Marvel Cinematic Universe (MCU) and serves as a direct sequel to 2012's "The Avengers" and 2015's "Avengers: Age of Ultron." The film features an ensemble cast of characters from previous MCU films.
65. Spider-Man : Into the Spider-Verse
"Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse" is a 2018 animated superhero film produced by Sony Pictures Animation and Marvel Entertainment. Directed by Bob Persichetti, Peter Ramsey, and Rodney Rothman, the film offers a fresh take on the Spider-Man mythos by introducing the concept of the multiverse.
66. The Shining
"The Shining," a 1980 psychological horror film directed by Stanley Kubrick. Based on the 1977 novel of the same name by Stephen King, the film stars Jack Nicholson, Shelley Duvall, and Danny Lloyd.
67. Witness for the Prosecution
"Witness for the Prosecution" is a 1957 courtroom drama film directed by Billy Wilder, based on the play of the same name by Agatha Christie. The film stars Tyrone Power, Marlene Dietrich, and Charles Laughton.
68. The Great Dictator
"The Great Dictator" is a 1940 American political satire comedy-drama film written, directed by, and starring Charlie Chaplin. It was Chaplin's first true sound film and is notable for its powerful political commentary, especially given its release during the rise of Adolf Hitler and Nazi Germany.
69. Aliens
"Aliens" is a 1986 science fiction action film directed by James Cameron and is the sequel to the 1979 film "Alien," directed by Ridley Scott. The film stars Sigourney Weaver reprising her role as Ellen Ripley, the sole survivor of the previous film's encounter with the hostile extraterrestrial creature known as the Xenomorph.
70. Inglourious Basterds
"Inglourious Basterds" is a 2009 war film written and directed by Quentin Tarantino. The film is set in Nazi-occupied France during World War II and follows several interconnected storylines involving a group of Jewish-American soldiers known as the "Basterds," a young Jewish woman seeking revenge for her family's murder, and a German officer involved in the propaganda machine.
71. The Dark Knight Rises
"The Dark Knight Rises" is the third and final installment in Christopher Nolan's Batman trilogy, following "Batman Begins" (2005) and "The Dark Knight" (2008). The film was released in 2012 and stars Christian Bale as Bruce Wayne/Batman, alongside an ensemble cast including Michael Caine, Gary Oldman, Anne Hathaway, Tom Hardy, Marion Cotillard, Joseph Gordon-Levitt, and Morgan Freeman.
72. Dr. Strangelove or: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Bomb
"Dr. Strangelove or: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Bomb" is a 1964 black comedy film directed by Stanley Kubrick. The film is a satirical take on the Cold War tensions between the United States and the Soviet Union during the 1960s and the looming threat of nuclear annihilation.
73. American Beauty
"American Beauty" is a 1999 American drama film directed by Sam Mendes and written by Alan Ball. The film stars Kevin Spacey as Lester Burnham, a middle-aged man going through a midlife crisis, and Annette Bening as his ambitious yet unhappy wife, Carolyn. The story revolves around their dysfunctional family life and the struggles of their teenage daughter, Jane, played by Thora Birch.
74. Oldboy
"Oldboy" is a South Korean psychological thriller film directed by Park Chan-wook, released in 2003. It is based on the Japanese manga of the same name written by Garon Tsuchiya and illustrated by Nobuaki Minegishi. The film stars Choi Min-sik, Yu Ji-tae, and Kang Hye-jung in the lead roles.
75. Coco
"Coco" is a 2017 animated film produced by Pixar Animation Studios and released by Walt Disney Pictures. Directed by Lee Unkrich and co-directed by Adrian Molina, the film is inspired by the Mexican holiday of Día de los Muertos (Day of the Dead).
76. Amadeus
"Amadeus" is a 1984 biographical drama film directed by Miloš Forman, based on the stage play of the same name by Peter Shaffer. The film portrays the life of Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, focusing particularly on his professional rivalry with fellow composer Antonio Salieri.
77. Toy Story
